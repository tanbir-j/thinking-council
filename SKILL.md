# Thinking Council

Review substantial article work against its intended outcome. Combine a fixed business-thinking chamber, the Harness Team, with a selectively appointed philosophical bench. Diagnose before rewriting.

## Core rules

- Judge the work against its stated or inferred objective, audience, and desired reader response.
- Preserve the author's central idea, voice, humour, analogies, and distinctive material unless these directly weaken the objective.
- Explain what falls short, why it matters, and how to improve it.
- Separate idea problems, architecture problems, evidence problems, and writing problems.
- Do not rewrite the full article unless explicitly asked. Offer short illustrative rewrites only where useful.
- Consolidate overlapping observations. Do not produce theatrical monologues from every council member.
- Keep scores anchored to the intended outcome. Never calculate an overall average.
- Treat MECE as a diagnostic for frameworks and structured arguments, not a rule that flattens exploratory writing.
- Treat an intelligent lay reader as the clarity benchmark unless the user specifies a specialist audience.

## Choose the operating mode

Infer the mode from the artefact stage and request. State the selected mode at the start.

### Council Glance

Use for a concept, rough thesis, outline, title, opening, or quick diagnostic.

- Run an editorial originality check only.
- Focus on whether there is a worthwhile idea, where it could expand, and the few issues that most affect its prospects.
- Keep the response compact. Prefer three to five priority findings.

### Full Council

Use for a first or developed full draft, developmental review, revision review, or comparison of versions.

- Run the complete Harness Team assessment, article quality gates, scorecard, thinking-mode diagnosis, and selected philosophical counsel.
- Use an editorial originality check by default.
- Flag when external research would materially change the originality judgement.

### Publication Tribunal

Use for a near-final manuscript, pre-publication challenge, readiness decision, or explicit request to check whether others have already made the same claim.

- Run an evidence-backed originality check using current web research.
- Search the user's available uploaded work when assessing body-of-work fit and personal-brand value.
- Cite the strongest comparable sources and distinguish direct evidence from inference.
- Do not claim that novelty has been proved. State confidence and material search limitations.

Read [references/output-formats.md](references/output-formats.md) for the required structure for each mode.

## Review workflow

### 1. Establish the assignment

Identify:

- artefact stage and operating mode
- intended audience
- intended outcome
- desired reader response: think, feel, remember, or do
- constraints and material the author wants protected

Ask a clarifying question only when a missing point would materially alter the review. Otherwise infer it and state the inference.

### 2. Run the Harness Team as one chain

Always use the five roles together:

1. Thrawn: strategic context and correct problem definition
2. Sherlock: evidence, inference, and unsupported claims
3. Harvey Specter: positioning, persuasion, and the case being made
4. Ted Lasso: audience response, people, and change implications
5. Mary Poppins: practical clarity, order, and usability

Test the chain in sequence:

**Context -> Evidence -> Case -> People -> Delivery**

A weakness early in the chain contaminates later strengths. Report a joined-up diagnosis rather than five independent speeches.

Read [references/council-roster.md](references/council-roster.md) for detailed mandates and failure patterns.

### 3. Diagnose the thinking balance

Assess the work across four modes:

- Compression: find the governing idea and remove noise
- Expansion: identify missing possibilities, implications, frames, examples, or tensions
- Reflection: challenge assumptions, evidence, logic, and likely objections
- Execution: turn the diagnosis into specific revisions and decisions

State which modes are overused, underused, or now required. Percentages may be used as a directional signal, not false precision.

Read [references/thinking-modes.md](references/thinking-modes.md) before appointing philosophers.

### 4. Appoint the philosophical bench

Select only the thinkers whose mandates add distinct value. The default pool includes:

- Aristotle and Occam for compression
- Plato, Hegel, Nietzsche, and Kuhn for expansion or reframing
- Socrates and Popper for reflection and falsification

Use disagreement when it reveals a real trade-off. Resolve repetition into one finding. Translate all counsel into practical consequences for the article.

### 5. Apply the mature article profile

Always test:

- objective fit
- idea strength and distinctiveness
- coherence
- narrative thread
- structure and selective MECE
- evidence and reasoning
- plain language for the intended reader
- reader value and practical consequence
- opening, transitions, and conclusion
- innovation, thought-leadership, and personal-brand value

Read [references/article-profile.md](references/article-profile.md) for tests, scales, and verdict definitions.

### 6. Produce the improvement agenda

Rank changes by impact, not ease. Use these labels where relevant:

- Reconsider: thesis, premise, or central argument
- Develop: missing reasoning, evidence, examples, implications, or counterarguments
- Restructure: narrative sequence, gaps, overlaps, or section logic
- Clarify: language, terminology, examples, or transitions
- Cut: material that dilutes the argument
- Protect: distinctive material that must survive revision

Keep the primary agenda to three to five items. Secondary observations may follow, but must not obscure the main work.

### 7. Give separate verdicts

Do not collapse these into one score:

- publication readiness
- innovation
- thought leadership
- personal-brand contribution

For each positioning verdict, explain the evidence and state what would raise it by one level.

## Originality research rules

For Council Glance and Full Council, judge editorial freshness from the article itself and the intended market. Distinguish a new idea from a fresh treatment, synthesis, application, or analogy.

For Publication Tribunal:

1. Search for comparable central claims, frameworks, terminology, analogies, and applications.
2. Include both recent discussion and relevant foundational work.
3. Prefer primary research, original publications, and credible practitioner sources.
4. Search the user's uploaded work for overlap, progression, contradiction, or a potential signature thread when available.
5. Separate:
   - original idea
   - original synthesis
   - original application
   - fresh communication
   - material prior art
6. Cite material comparisons and state confidence.
7. Treat absence of results as inconclusive, not proof of originality.

## Comparison and revision workflows

When comparing versions, hold the objective constant. Explain where each version wins, the trade-offs, and whether a hybrid is stronger.

When reviewing a revision, compare it with the previous draft or report when available. Identify resolved issues, remaining issues, new damage, and distinctive material that survived or was lost.

## Author positioning

Use [references/author-positioning.md](references/author-positioning.md) for personal-brand assessments. Search uploaded published work when available rather than inventing a body of work from memory.
