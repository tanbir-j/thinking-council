# Mature Article Profile

## Purpose

Use this profile for article concepts, outlines, drafts, revisions, comparisons, and pre-publication reviews. The standard is publishable thought leadership, not mere competence.

## Review layers

### 1. The idea

Test whether there is something worth publishing:

- Is the central claim clear?
- Does it matter to the intended audience?
- Is it distinctive, defensible, and consequential?
- Is the contribution an idea, synthesis, application, framework, language, or analogy?
- Has the idea been expanded far enough to reveal implications and tensions?

### 2. The architecture

Test whether the article carries the idea:

- Does the thesis remain stable?
- Does one narrative thread run from opening to conclusion?
- Does every section advance that thread?
- Are sections sequenced at the right logical level?
- Are frameworks complete enough and non-overlapping where MECE is useful?
- Do recommendations follow from the analysis?
- Does the conclusion resolve or advance the opening question?

### 3. The reader experience

Test whether an intelligent intended reader can follow and retain it:

- Is the language simple without becoming thin?
- Are specialist terms removed, explained, or earned?
- Do examples and analogies explain rather than decorate?
- Does the pace hold attention?
- Are the main ideas memorable and reusable?
- Does the reader know what to think or do afterwards?

## Permanent quality gates

### Coherence

Check whether the article feels like one argument rather than several good observations sharing a document.

Flag:

- thesis drift
- contradictions between sections
- examples that pull away from the claim
- recommendations unsupported by the diagnosis
- sections that belong in another article

### Plain language

Use an intelligent lay reader as the default benchmark.

Flag:

- unexplained terminology
- abstract nouns stacked together
- sentences carrying too many ideas
- impressive-sounding phrases with little content
- assumed specialist knowledge
- simplification that damages precision

### Narrative thread

State the narrative thread in one sentence. If it cannot be stated, record that as a material finding.

Test:

1. The opening establishes the thread.
2. Each section develops it.
3. Transitions explain why the next section follows.
4. The conclusion completes or productively opens the thread.
5. Interesting detours do not sever it.

### MECE and structural integrity

Apply MECE where the article presents categories, stages, causes, components, options, or a numbered framework.

Ask:

- Do categories overlap?
- Is anything material omitted?
- Are items at the same logical level?
- Is one item a subset of another?
- Have unlike things been combined to reach a pleasing number?
- Has reality been forced into a tidy model?

Allow deliberate recurrence, ambiguity, and tension in essays where these serve the narrative.

## Scorecard

Score each dimension from 1 to 5 against the stated objective. Give a brief reason and no overall average.

| Dimension | Core question |
| --- | --- |
| Objective fit | Will the article achieve what the author wants? |
| Idea strength | Is the central claim meaningful, defensible, and worth publishing? |
| Distinctiveness | Does it add a recognisable perspective? |
| Coherence | Do argument, evidence, and implications form one whole? |
| Narrative thread | Can the reader follow a clear line from opening to conclusion? |
| Structural integrity | Are sections and frameworks logically arranged, sufficiently complete, and non-overlapping where required? |
| Clarity | Can the intended reader understand it without unnecessary effort? |
| Reader value | Will the reader remember, reconsider, or do something? |

### Scale

- 1: Fundamentally misses the objective
- 2: Material weaknesses
- 3: Sound but ordinary or uneven
- 4: Strong and largely ready
- 5: Exceptional for its intended purpose

A high score in one dimension does not compensate for a fatal weakness elsewhere.

## Publication readiness verdict

- Not ready: rethink the proposition
- Promising: substantive development required
- Strong: targeted revision required
- Ready: minor editorial changes only

## Innovation verdict

Judge relative to the intended audience and current conversation, not against every idea ever published.

- Conventional: familiar ideas with limited new contribution
- Fresh treatment: familiar subject presented through a stronger frame or application
- Distinctive synthesis: known ingredients combined into a recognisable new perspective
- Original contribution: a new model, argument, or method with practical value
- Category-shaping: could change how the subject is discussed or approached

Always state where the innovation resides.

## Thought-leadership verdict

- Commentary: a view on an existing discussion
- Useful synthesis: known material organised clearly
- Practitioner insight: experience-based observations others can apply
- Thought leadership: a distinctive, credible, consequential point of view
- Signature thinking: an idea or framework likely to become associated with the author

Test for a clear position, earned authority, practical consequence, reusable language, and resilience under informed challenge.

## Personal-brand verdict

- Dilutes the brand
- Brand neutral
- Reinforces authority
- Extends the territory
- Signature brand asset

Test territory, authority, recognition, and cumulative value. Explain what would move the article up one level.

## Stage-specific originality

### Concept and outline

Run an editorial originality check. Explore how the proposition could become more distinctive before exposing it to a full prior-art search.

### Full draft

Run an editorial originality check by default. Recommend evidence-backed research when the originality claim is central or uncertain.

### Near-final or pre-publication

Run an evidence-backed check. Distinguish original idea, synthesis, application, communication, and prior art. State confidence and cite comparisons.

## Article-stage use cases

### Idea stage

Assess whether the observation can sustain an article, where conventional thinking begins, and what tensions or implications could enlarge it.

### Outline stage

Test whether the structure proves the thesis, sections build rather than repeat, and the evidence plan is adequate.

### Full draft

Separate flaws in the idea, architecture, evidence, and writing. Protect distinctive material.

### Pre-publication

Stress-test claims, overstatement, likely objections, originality, citations, and the publication decision.

### Revision

Confirm what improved, what remains, what regressed, and whether the article is now ready.

### Comparison

Compare alternatives against the same objective. Show trade-offs and hybrid options.
